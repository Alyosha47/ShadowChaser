About

ShadowChaser is developed by followtheshadow, a loose consortium of friends who have been following eclipses together since May 10, 1994. Although there are many eclipse-adventure-planning tools out there, we still missed certain features — search-by-location, accurate local timezone resolution, historical weather planning, shadow-mapping from local topographies, and offline map caching for in-the-field use. We’ve tried to cover all the bases in one convenient package.

Tuned to iPhone and never tested on Android — let us know if you have any suggestions. If you find any bugs, drop us a line — and don’t stare at the sun!

Sources

Eclipse catalogue and Besselian elements are sourced from Espenak & Meeus, Five Millennium Canon of Solar Eclipses: −1999 to +3000 (NASA/TP-2006-214141). Local circumstances are computed via the Besselian element method described by Jean Meeus in Astronomical Algorithms (Willmann-Bell, 2nd ed. 1998, Ch. 54). No lunar limb correction is applied.

Delta T (ΔT = TT − UT) drifts with the Earth’s rotation and has its own delta over time. This code uses the best available source per date: 1973 to last month — USNO observed monthly tables (deltat.data); last month to ~5 years out — USNO quarterly forecast (deltat.preds); −720 to 2050 (outside the USNO range) — Espenak–Meeus piecewise polynomial; before −720 and after 2050 — Stephenson, Morrison & Hohenkerk (2016) LOD integral extrapolation.

Basemaps are served from OpenFreeMap (Liberty vector style, openfreemap.org) when online, and from Natural Earth public-domain geodata (naturalearthdata.com) when offline.

Timezones are resolved offline via the tz-lookup polygon database. Elevation is auto-filled by the Open-Elevation API when online.
